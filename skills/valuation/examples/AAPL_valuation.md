# Apple Inc. (AAPL) — Enterprise DCF

As of 2026-05-29 | billions USD | Method: McKinsey enterprise DCF (midyear convention)

## Verdict

Intrinsic value of about **USD 140.53 per share** against a market price of **USD 312.06**. On a disciplined enterprise DCF, Apple looks **overvalued by roughly 55%**. The market is pricing in materially faster long-run growth, a higher sustainable return on incremental capital, or a lower discount rate than a conservative McKinsey-style model supports.

## Thesis

Apple is an elite-ROIC franchise: about USD 133B of FY2025 operating income on a modest invested-capital base, with strong incremental returns (assumed RONIC 18%, comfortably above the 8.7% cost of capital), so growth still creates value. The disconnect is not the quality of the business but the price: even generous cash-flow growth and a high terminal return discount back to roughly USD 140, less than half the current quote.

## Key drivers (what the value hinges on)

- **Long-run free cash flow growth.** Explicit FCF rises from about USD 105B (FY2026) to USD 156B (FY2035), tapering toward a 2.5% terminal rate.
- **Continuing value.** Key-value-driver form with terminal growth 2.5% and RONIC 18%. CV is 57.5% of operating value, within a healthy range (below the 75% stress threshold).
- **WACC 8.67%.** Cost of equity 8.77% (risk-free 4.0%, ERP 4.5%, beta 1.06), after-tax cost of debt 3.78%, equity weight 97.9% (Apple is almost entirely equity-financed at market value).

## Sensitivity (value per share, WACC by terminal growth)

At plausible bounds the value stays well below the market price. A bullish corner (WACC 7.67%, g 3.5%) reaches about USD 184; a bearish corner (WACC 9.67%, g 1.5%) about USD 117. The market price of USD 312 sits outside this entire grid, which tells you the gap is structural, not a knife-edge assumption.

## Risks and what would change the call

- **Higher sustainable growth.** A durable AI-driven services and hardware upgrade supercycle could justify faster long-run growth than 2.5%, but even 3.5% terminal growth does not close the gap on its own.
- **Lower discount rate.** If one anchors cost of equity nearer the bottom of the modern range, value rises, but not to USD 312.
- **Reinvestment runway.** A higher and more durable RONIC on a larger reinvestment base would lift continuing value; the model already credits an above-cost-of-capital 18%.
- **Buybacks do not create value** by themselves (conservation of value); they change share count and claims, not the underlying cash flows.
