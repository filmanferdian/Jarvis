# Step 0 — Foundations of Value Creation (McKinsey Valuation, 4th ed., Ch 3 & 6)

> Pure 4th-edition book methodology, extracted via NotebookLM. Modern refinements live in `modern-updates.md`.

## Purpose
These chapters establish the single idea the whole DCF rests on: companies create value by investing capital at rates of return that exceed their cost of capital, and value equals the present value of the expected future cash flows those returns produce. ROIC, growth, and the cost of capital are all that matter; "everything else is mere detail."

## Key concepts
- **Core principle:** "Companies create value by investing capital at rates of return that exceed their cost of capital." Value is the present value of expected future cash flows.
- Cash flows are driven by exactly two operating factors: **Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)** and **Growth (g)**.
  - **ROIC** determines how much profit is generated for every dollar invested.
  - **Growth** is the rate at which profits and cash flows increase each year.
- A company that earns higher returns needs **less investment to achieve the same growth**, which produces **higher cash flow** and therefore greater value. Book wording: "A greater return on invested capital results in more cash flow, given the same growth rate in operating profits."
- The key value driver formula is called the **"Zen of Corporate Finance"** because it relates value directly to the fundamental drivers of economic value.
- **Value driver tree intuition (Ch 6):** high ROIC must be justified by at least one source of competitive advantage:
  - **Price premium** — differentiation lets the firm charge above marginal cost.
  - **Cost competitiveness** — lower cost than competitors (e.g., Wal-Mart).
  - **Capital efficiency** — more product sold per dollar of invested capital (e.g., Southwest Airlines).
  - **Sustainability** — the longer these advantages resist competition, the greater the value.

## Formulas

**Key value driver formula ("Zen of Corporate Finance") — general form:**

    Value = NOPLAT(t=1) × (1 − g / ROIC) / (WACC − g)

**Continuing-value / new-capital form (uses RONIC for incremental capital):**

    Continuing Value(t) = NOPLAT(t+1) × (1 − g / RONIC) / (WACC − g)

**Growth / investment / return relationship:**

    Growth Rate = Return on New Invested Capital × Investment Rate
    g = ROIC × IR
    IR = g / ROIC

**Free cash flow identities:**

    FCF = NOPLAT − Net Investment
    FCF = NOPLAT × (1 − Investment Rate)

**ROIC definition and value driver tree decomposition (Ch 6):**

    ROIC = NOPLAT / Invested Capital
    ROIC = (1 − T) × [(Unit Price − Unit Cost) × Quantity] / Invested Capital

**Term definitions (as the book states them):**
- **NOPLAT** (Net Operating Profits Less Adjusted Taxes): the profits generated from a company's core operations after subtracting income taxes related to those core operations.
- **g (Growth):** the rate at which the company's NOPLAT and cash flow grow each year.
- **ROIC (Return on Invested Capital):** "the return the company earns on each dollar invested in the business"; defined as NOPLAT divided by invested capital. In the basic formula ROIC and the return on new capital are often assumed equal.
- **RONIC (Return on New Invested Capital):** the "expected rate of return on new (or incremental) invested capital," specifically the return on incremental investments during the continuing-value period.
- **WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital):** the rate of return investors expect from investing in the company; used as the discount rate for free cash flow.
- **Invested Capital:** the cumulative amount the business has invested in its core operations, primarily working capital and property, plant, and equipment.
- **Investment Rate (IR):** "the portion of NOPLAT invested back into the business"; calculated as net investment divided by operating profits (NOPLAT).
- **Net Investment:** "the increase in invested capital from one year to the next."
- **Free Cash Flow (FCF):** the after-tax cash flow generated by the core operations available to all investors after deducting investments in new capital.

## How value is created (decision rules)
Growth does not always create value; its impact depends on the **spread between ROIC and WACC**:
- **ROIC > WACC** → growth **creates** value (the higher the growth, the more value created).
- **ROIC = WACC** → growth is **value-neutral** (additional growth neither creates nor destroys value).
- **ROIC < WACC** → growth **destroys** value (investors would earn better returns elsewhere; faster growth makes it worse).

**Conservation of value:** "anything that does not increase cash flows does not create value." Value is *conserved* when it is merely moved around or relabeled without changing expected future cash flows. Changing capital structure or repurchasing shares only changes the **claims** on value or the **timing** of payouts; unless an action changes total **NOPLAT** or the **net investment** needed for growth, total enterprise value is unchanged. Book examples:
- **Acquisitions to boost EPS (EG Corporation / Woodco):** the market "sees through" acquisitions structured to avoid EPS dilution; EG's share-price decline around the acquisitions equaled the premiums paid because the market saw no real synergies.
- **Share repurchases:** "repurchasing shares will mechanically increase EPS regardless of value creation," but it does not improve underlying operating performance or total cash flow, so it creates no value.
- **Financial engineering:** EG's CEO declines securitization and leasing schemes because, for an investment-grade company, they are "unlikely to create real shareholder value" and only distract from operations.
- **Accounting changes:** the market knows "the accounting treatment of goodwill amortization does not affect cash flows"; when U.S. GAAP abolished goodwill amortization, affected share prices returned to normal within two weeks.
- **Different accounting standards:** firms under different standards (U.S. GAAP vs. domestic) do not have different values because "investors care about underlying performance."

## Worked example (from the book)
**Value, Inc. vs. Volume, Inc.** Both earn **$100 NOPLAT in Year 1**; WACC = 10% for all cases.

| Metric | Volume, Inc. | Value, Inc. (5% growth) | Value, Inc. (8% growth) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| NOPLAT (Year 1) | $100 | $100 | $100 |
| Growth rate (g) | 5% | 5% | 8% |
| ROIC | 10% | 20% | 20% |
| WACC | 10% | 10% | 10% |
| Investment Rate (g/ROIC) | 50% | 25% | 40% |
| Free Cash Flow (Year 1) | $50 | $75 | $60 |
| Resulting Value | $1,000 | $1,500 | $3,000 |

Key insights:
- **Value beats Volume:** with identical earnings and identical 5% growth, Value, Inc. is worth $1,500 vs. Volume, Inc.'s $1,000, because its higher ROIC (20% vs. 10%) lets it invest less (25% vs. 50% of NOPLAT) and generate more cash flow ($75 vs. $50) for the same growth. Book wording: "Value, Inc.'s cash flows are higher than Volume, Inc.'s despite identical profits because it invests less than Volume, Inc. to achieve the same profit growth."
- **Growth pays only when ROIC > WACC:** for Value, Inc. (ROIC 20% > WACC 10%), raising growth from 5% to 8% doubles value from $1,500 to $3,000 — even though near-term cash flow is lower ($60 vs. $75). The same growth increase would not help Volume, Inc., whose ROIC merely equals WACC.

## What this means for the DCF workflow
- Forecast and discipline every forecast around **ROIC and growth** — these two drivers, with WACC, fully determine value; do not be distracted by accounting cosmetics.
- Test each year's economics with the FCF identity (`FCF = NOPLAT × (1 − g/ROIC)`): growth only adds value where ROIC exceeds WACC, so model the ROIC–WACC spread explicitly.
- Build the continuing value using the **RONIC** form, since long-run value depends on the return on *incremental* capital, which may differ from the historical ROIC.
- Treat value as conserved: capital-structure tweaks, buybacks, and accounting changes should not move your enterprise value unless they change NOPLAT or required net investment.
