# Step 2 — Analyze Historical Performance (McKinsey Valuation, 4th ed., Ch 7)

> Pure 4th-edition book methodology, extracted via NotebookLM. Modern refinements live in `modern-updates.md`.

## Purpose
Return on invested capital (ROIC) is the single best measure for evaluating a company's operational performance because it is independent of financial structure and nonoperating assets. The goal of historical analysis is to understand what drives a company's value: how profitably it deploys capital (ROIC) and how fast it grows (revenue growth), then to assess whether recent performance is permanent, temporary, or merely an accounting effect.

## ROIC (formulas, with/without goodwill)

**Core formula:**

```
ROIC = NOPLAT / Invested Capital
```

- **NOPLAT** (Net Operating Profit Less Adjusted Taxes): the total after-tax operating income generated by the company's core operations that is available to all financial investors.
- **Invested Capital**: the cumulative amount the business has invested in its core operations — primarily property, plant, and equipment (PP&E) and working capital.
- **Averaging capital**: Because profit is measured over a year but capital is a point-in-time measurement, use **average starting and ending invested capital** to avoid overestimating returns if new assets were acquired during the year.

**With vs. without goodwill** — compute both, because each gives a different perspective:

- **ROIC including goodwill** — "measures historical performance for the company's shareholders." It accounts for the actual resources spent to acquire businesses and shows whether management is properly compensating investors for price premiums paid in acquisitions. A company that pays a high premium but cannot generate high enough returns on that total investment destroys shareholder value.
- **ROIC excluding goodwill** (excluding goodwill and acquired intangibles) — "measures the company's internal performance." It is the most useful metric for **comparing operating performance across companies** and for analyzing trends, because it is not distorted by varying price premiums paid for past acquisitions.

## ROIC decomposition tree

ROIC is driven by the company's ability to maximize profitability, optimize capital efficiency, or minimize taxes:

```
ROIC = (1 - Operating Cash Tax Rate) × (EBITA / Revenues) × (Revenues / Invested Capital)
     = (1 - Operating Cash Tax Rate) × Operating Margin × Capital Turnover
```

Full tree with sub-drivers:

- **Tax effect**: `(1 - Operating Cash Tax Rate)`
- **Pre-tax ROIC** = **Operating Margin × Capital Turnover**
  - **Operating Margin** = `EBITA / Revenues`
    - **Gross Margin** = `(Revenue - COGS) / Revenue`
    - **SG&A / Revenues**
    - **Depreciation / Revenues**
  - **Capital Turnover (Turns)** = `Revenues / Invested Capital`
    - **Fixed Assets / Revenues** — revenues per dollar of investment in physical assets like PP&E
    - **Operating Working Capital / Revenues** — efficiency in managing current assets and liabilities

Disaggregating this way reveals whether a company's advantage comes from superior pricing / cost control (**Operating Margin**) or from using its assets more efficiently than competitors (**Capital Turnover**).

## Revenue growth analysis

Year-to-year revenue growth taken straight from the income statement suffices for most companies, but it can be misleading because of **currency fluctuations, mergers and acquisitions (M&A), and changes in accounting policies**.

Reported revenue growth decomposes into four components:
1. **Organic revenue growth** — growth "attributable to the company's core business, independent of currency fluctuations, acquisitions and divestitures, and accounting changes."
2. **Currency effects**
3. **Acquisitions** (and divestitures)
4. **Accounting changes**

**Computing organic growth** — strip the other three effects from reported figures. Example (IBM 2003): compare the year's **constant-currency revenue** to a **pro forma** prior-year revenue that includes partial-year revenue from acquisitions, so the years are comparable.

Distortions and recommended adjustments:
- **Currency**: Multinational revenues are converted to home currency at period end. A weakening home currency raises reported revenue even with no increase in pricing power or volume. Use **"constant currency"** changes — translate current-period results using prior-year exchange rates.
- **Acquisitions / divestitures**: Bidders often include only partial-year revenues for a target after a deal closes, which distorts ROIC and historical comparisons. Build **pro forma statements that recast historical financials as though the acquisition was completed at the beginning of the fiscal year.** For consistency, if only 10 months of a target's revenue are in the current year, add 10 months of the target's historical revenue to the prior year.

## Cash flow & credit health

**Free cash flow (FCF)** — the "after-tax cash flow available to all investors: debt holders and equity holders," calculated as if the company held only core operating assets and was financed entirely with equity:

```
FCF = NOPLAT + Noncash Operating Expenses - Investments in Invested Capital
FCF = NOPLAT - Net Increase in Invested Capital
```

- **Gross Cash Flow** = NOPLAT + Noncash operating expenses (e.g. depreciation, employee stock options) — "cash flow generated by the company's operations."
- **Gross Investment** (subtracted from gross cash flow): increase in operating working capital, **Net capital expenditures** (change in net PP&E + depreciation), Change in capitalized operating leases, and Investment in acquired intangibles and goodwill.

**Cash flow available to investors** — combines FCF with cash flows from nonoperating assets to show total cash available to all financial claimants:
- **Origin of cash**: Free cash flow + After-tax interest income (from excess cash / marketable securities) + Decrease (increase) in other nonoperating assets.
- **Disposition of cash (must equal total financing flow)**: After-tax interest expenses, Debt issues and repurchases, Dividends, Share issues and repurchases, Change in debt and equity equivalents (e.g. pension liabilities).

**Credit health** — assessed through liquidity (coverage) and leverage.

Coverage ratios (liquidity):
- **Interest coverage**: `EBITA / Interest` or `EBITDA / Interest`
- **EBITDAR coverage**: `EBITDAR / (Interest Expense + Rental Expense)` — an "advanced measure" that includes rental expense to account for operating leases.
- **Debt coverage**: `Net Debt / EBITDA` (used in case studies to measure ability to service debt).

Leverage ratios (financial strength):
- **Market debt-to-equity**: `(Market) Debt / Equity`
- **Debt-to-capitalization**: `Debt / Total Book Capitalization` and `Debt / Total Market Capitalization` (capitalization = debt + equity).
- **ROE leverage formula**: `ROE = ROIC + [ROIC - (1 - T)k_d] × (D/E)`, where `k_d` is the after-tax cost of debt and `D/E` is the book-based debt-to-equity ratio.

How to read them:
- **EBITA / Interest** — ability to "repay interest using profits without having to cut expenditures intended to replace depreciating equipment."
- **EBITDA / Interest** — ability to "meet short-term financial commitments" using both current profits and depreciation dollars earmarked for replacement capital.
- **EBITDAR / (Interest + Rental)** — ability to meet "known future obligations, including the effect of operating leases."
- **Leverage (D/E)** — ability to "meet obligations over the long term." A higher ratio makes ROE more "sensitive to changes in operating performance (ROIC)" and increases shareholder risk.
- **Payout vs. reinvestment** — a high dividend payout with a cash flow reinvestment ratio greater than 1 means the company is "borrowing money to fund negative free cash flow, to pay interest, or to pay dividends," which is often unsustainable.

## Step-by-step procedure
1. Reorganize standard financial statements into **operating vs. nonoperating** categories (separates core performance from capital-structure effects).
2. Compute **ROIC** (NOPLAT / average invested capital), both **with and without goodwill**.
3. Disaggregate ROIC down the decomposition tree: tax effect, operating margin (gross margin, SG&A/revenues, depreciation/revenues) and capital turnover (fixed assets/revenues, operating working capital/revenues).
4. Decompose **revenue growth** into organic, currency, acquisition, and accounting-change components; restate to constant currency and build pro forma statements where needed.
5. Analyze **free cash flow** and **cash flow available to investors**.
6. Assess **credit health** via coverage and leverage ratios.
7. Look back **as far as possible (at least 10 years)** to see whether performance reverts to a normal level and whether recent trends are permanent or temporary.

## Common pitfalls (from the book)
- Using **point-in-time** instead of **average** invested capital, which overestimates returns when assets were added during the year.
- Reading raw income-statement revenue growth without stripping **currency, M&A, and accounting-change** distortions.
- Ignoring the **with-goodwill vs. without-goodwill** distinction — mixing shareholder performance with internal operating performance.
- Failing to convert **nonoperating restructuring provisions** from an accrual to a cash basis and treat them as nonoperating.
- Not **capitalizing operating leases** (recognize as both an operating asset and a debt-equivalent liability) for cross-firm comparability.
- Failing to review the **MD&A** to catch material accounting changes and recast historical statements where necessary.
- Analyzing too short a history; trends look different over 10+ years.

## Inputs needed / Outputs produced
- **Inputs**: reorganized NOPLAT, invested capital, revenue history (from Step 1)
- **Outputs**: ROIC trend (with and without goodwill), growth decomposition (organic / currency / acquisitions / accounting), margin and capital-turnover drivers, credit-health read (coverage + leverage)
